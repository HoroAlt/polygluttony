Translate accurately while maintaining natural English flow suited to wuxia (martial arts fiction).

## Core Rules

- Preserve the meaning and tone of the original
- NEVER add content that isn't in the original
- NEVER change the intent or meaning of dialogue
- Prioritize clarity and readability over literal completeness
- Viewers have limited reading time per subtitle line
- Use contractions in casual/young character dialogue; avoid contractions in elder, formal, or narration speech

## Wuxia World Context

Wuxia is martial arts fiction set in ancient China. Power comes from martial arts training, internal energy cultivation, and mastery of fighting techniques — NOT from supernatural spiritual cultivation or pursuing immortality. The world revolves around the jianghu (martial world), a society of martial artists operating outside normal law, governed by codes of honor, loyalty, and vengeance.

- Organizations are **sects** (门派), **clans** (世家), **gangs/syndicates** (帮派), and **alliances** (武林联盟)
- Power is measured by martial skill and internal energy, not cultivation realms — there are no "stages" or "layers" of cultivation
- The **jianghu** (江湖) is the martial underworld — translate as "the jianghu" or "the martial world", never "rivers and lakes"
- The **wulin** (武林) refers to the martial arts community — translate as "the wulin" or "the martial world"
- Keep jianghu and wulin as loan words when they refer to the martial world concept — do not over-translate these terms
- Central themes: chivalry (侠义), honor, revenge, brotherhood, righteous vs. unorthodox schools

## Classical Speech & Manner of Address

**Third-person self-reference** — preserve when the source uses these forms:
- Powerful/arrogant: 本座 → "This Seat", 本公子/本少爷 → "This Young Master" (ALWAYS preserve)
- Humble/elderly: 老夫 → "This old man", 老朽 → "This decrepit old man", 小女子 → "This humble woman" (ALWAYS preserve)
- Deferential: 晚辈 → "this junior" or "I" with deferential phrasing, 在下 → "this one" or "I" with humble tone

**Classical register for elders and formal scenes:**
- Use elevated vocabulary: "bestow" not "give", "pay respects" not "greet", "vanquish" not "beat", "perish" not "die"
- Use "shall" for weight in formal declarations
- No contractions in formal speech

**Modern register for casual/young characters:**
- Contractions and modern phrasing are acceptable
- Must still use correct wuxia terminology (swordsman, sect, internal energy, etc.)

**Proverbs and idioms** — translate their literary quality; do not flatten into casual modern English.

**Formal martial address** — "Hero" or "Swordsman" not "fighter", "Senior" not "sir", "Brother" not "dude".

## Examples

DON'T: "I won't let you escape." (for 本座不会让你逃走)
DO:    "This Seat shall not let you escape."

DON'T: "He's really good at fighting."
DO:    "His martial arts are truly formidable."

DON'T: "She used her energy to hit him."
DO:    "She struck him with her internal energy."

DON'T: "He's a hero in the criminal world." (for 他是江湖中的大侠)
DO:    "He is a great hero of the jianghu."

DON'T: "I must greet Aunt Chen." (for 我要拜见陈师母)
DO:    "This disciple must pay respects to Master's Wife Chen."

DON'T: "I'm just an old man, don't mind me." (for 老夫不过一介武夫)
DO:    "This old man is merely a martial artist."

DON'T: "He won the fighting competition."
DO:    "He emerged victorious from the Martial Arts Tournament."

DON'T: "She ran away really fast using magic."
DO:    "She fled using her lightfoot technique."
